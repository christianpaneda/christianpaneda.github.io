CHAI website: redesign for health equity

Website   Mobile Design   Work Project

Communities have the power to drive change. Redesigning the CHAI website is an opportunity to amplify LGBTQIA+ voices.


Role: Lead UX Designer & Web Developer


Skills: User Interviewing, Affinity Mapping, User Testing, Branding, UX Design, Prototyping, Web Development


Project Type: Job Project


Timeline: September - Present (4 Months)


Tools Used: Excel, Figma, Miro, Zoom, Blush.Design

Overview

The Collective Health Access Initiative (CHAI) is a coalition of community organizations, youth, and academic partners offering free trainings and technical assistance programs to improve the health and well-being of LGBTQIA+ youth in Washtenaw County, Michigan. CHAI is focused on improving mental health and substance use outcomes among LGBTQ+ youth in Washtenaw County by increasing access to inclusive and affirming health care. University partners represent various departments and are experts in psychology, social work, psychiatry, and LGBTQ+ physical and mental health. The Community Health Access Initiative (CHAI) is a project from the Resistance and Resilience Collective within the University of Michigan School of Public Health (UM-SPH).


Currently, CHAI has a live website to direct providers, youth, and allies to their LGBTQIA+ health-related resources and training. However, the website has many accessibility issues related to color contrasts and coding semantics.


As the lead UX Designer & Web Developer, I was hired to redesign and develop a new website for CHAI. I worked under the University of Michigan School of Information (UMSI) faculty while working with CHAI staff from UM-SPH as well as the 5-member CHAI Action Committee of young professionals who identify as LGBTQIA+ community members and served as users. This case study highlights the design of the mobile-version of the website.


How might I partner with public health experts, providers, and LGBTQIA+ coalitions to better communicate health care resources and trainings in a website by leveraging community partnerships for user-centered design?

Design Overview

Decision #1: Create a landing page contains mission of CHAI website, resources, and events Users are able to learn more about CHAI faster. If the user is lost, they can return to a landing page to see other available information that is more descriptive than a hamburger menu. The different ways of accessing the same information will appease to different use cases. More information on the landing page = less interactions for users to try to learn more about CHAI

Decision #2: Create a resource page with a filter Users can find specific resources faster Users have the choice to view all resources or ones relevant to their needs

Decision #3: Split up the provider, resources, and events page, and contact page Organizes the information architecture of the website Makes it easier for users to understand which resources are relevant to them

Decision #4: Create cards to illustrate and separate information Imagery helps the user understand specific health care content The organization of the card makes information more understandable, especially on a mobile device

Design Process

I followed a five-phase design thinking process to assist my client with their needs. This is a long-term (1+ year) project, so I have iterated over the entire process several times to integrate feedback in the designs and include community input.

Initial Key Findings

  1. Users need to a way to search and ask for relevant LGBTQIA+ health care resources within the website

  2. Users need a solution to be accessible in its design such as color contrasts, code semantics, and availability among devices

  3. The website has multiple audiences. It should be useful to not only LGBTQIA+ youth but also providers

  4. Users want the want to be diverse beyond solely LGBTQIA+ identity (e.g. race, ethnicity, body type)

User Research

Users interviews and testing are conducted every other month to build strong working relationships and to design the website with the community.


So far, I have conducted a first-round of qualitative user interviews with all five of the CHAI Action Committee members to understand their needs as young LBGTQIA+ community members and explore the user experience of the website. Interviews were conducted via online video call so that they could share their screens and lasted between 45 minutes to 1 hour.


I created an interview protocol to guide conversations about user needs and to conduct user testing with the mobile version of the website. Themes explored in the interviews include: LGBTQIA+ inclusion, LGBTQIA+ health care needs, accessibility of the prototyped website, and overall feedback on the visual components of the website.

Vision & Brand

The client wanted a playful aesthetic, something similar to video games for children. The data verified that users would enjoy the same aesthetic as many enjoy video games and language learning apps for younger/beginner audiences. The client and I brainstormed a mood board to further conceptualize a vision for the products. Therefore, I came up with a brand design that is fun, simple, and almost child-like. I believe it appeals to a diverse audience of young adults.

The brand design includes a warm color scheme, typography, and logo. I used Figma and Procreate. The predominately orange color represents the optimistic tone of the products, and the playful appearance of Nunito Sans conveys a sense of encouragement for users to learn the language.

Deliverables

Though brainstorming and the user research, the client and I determined the following outcomes for the project:

  1. Conceptualize and develop a responsive mobile website to house the client’s teaching materials such as videos and worksheets. The website should also include information about the non-profit and how to contact the client

  2. Prototype a companion mobile app to address user needs to improve speaking and listening skills while making the experience interactive

Wireframing

The client gave me complete freedom of all the layouts. For a minimal yet optimal flow, I wanted the website to be one page. The user can click the navigation bar to skip ahead to each of the sections.

The mobile app accounted for the fact that users wanted visual, auditory, and tactile elements in their experiences. I envisioned a main menu screen for the app to better organize the lessons and resources. There are five sections to the app: “Videos”, “Play”, “Learn”, “Tips”, and “Club.” The video section fulfills the users’ visual needs. The “Play” and “Learn” sections fulfill the users’ auditory learning needs as they check for the users’ correct pronunciation of words or phrases. The “Play” section also addresses tactile learning elements as users will play games as a means for learning.


The “Tips” section provides positive reinforcement for users: an important concern found in the user research. The “Club” section allows users to interact and video chat with each other to practice listening and speaking skills.

This is the first project where I made a prototype of a mobile app. I definitely learned that there are different practices when designing mobile apps versus web pages. In the future, I hope to design more clean designs. I am starting graduate school soon where I will be studying UX Research and Design. I hope to learn more about the design thinking process, consulting skills, and visual design best practices to create more accessible and professional products.
